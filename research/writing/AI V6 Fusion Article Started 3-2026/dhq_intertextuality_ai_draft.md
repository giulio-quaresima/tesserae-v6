## Abstract

The computational detection of intertextual relationships in classical literature has relied predominantly on lexical matching — identifying shared words or dictionary headwords between passages. While effective for a subset of literary allusions, this approach cannot capture the full range of signals through which ancient poets evoked their predecessors: rhythm, sound, conceptual resonance, syntactic structure, and rare vocabulary. This article presents Tesserae V6, a freely available web platform that advances computational intertextuality through *feature fusion*, the systematic combination of multiple independent search channels into a unified detection and scoring framework. The channels span lexical matching, phonetic similarity, character-level fuzzy matching, semantic embeddings from a neural language model, curated synonym lookup, syntactic dependency pattern matching, and rare vocabulary detection. A weighted scoring layer combines channel outputs with a convergence bonus that rewards cross-channel agreement and a rarity-aware system that suppresses common-word noise. The platform supports Latin, Greek, and English texts, with cross-lingual search across Greek-Latin, Latin-English, and Greek-English pairs. Evaluated against five scholar-curated benchmark datasets comprising 862 gold-standard parallel passages from Latin epic, the system achieves 91.9% recall, nearly quadrupling the approximately 27% recall of the original Tesserae system. Evaluated against a Greek benchmark of 121 strong parallels between Apollonius and Homer (Hunter 1989), the same architecture achieves 69.4% recall, with the gap reflecting the current state of Greek NLP resources rather than architectural limitations. Cross-lingual Greek-Latin detection, evaluated against Knauer's (1964) catalog of 412 Iliad-Aeneid parallels, achieves 94.4% absolute recall across four channels. The work incorporates two AI-enabled approaches to this longstanding problem: AI as a detection method, through a neural language model whose learned representations identify semantic parallels between passages sharing no vocabulary; and AI as a development tool, through an extended collaboration between a humanities scholar and AI coding assistants that produced the platform without traditional programming. The platform is freely available at https://tesserae.caset.buffalo.edu, with source code at https://github.com/tesserae/tesserae-v6 and supporting data at https://tesserae.caset.buffalo.edu/downloads.

## Introduction

In 2012, "Intertextuality in the Digital Age" introduced the Tesserae Project's approach to automated intertext detection: a system that compared passages of Latin poetry by shared dictionary headwords, recovering approximately a quarter of known allusions between Vergil and Lucan (Coffee et al. 2012). Six years later, "An Agenda for the Study of Intertextuality" (Coffee 2018) outlined a research program for the field, calling for benchmark standards, multi-feature detection beyond purely lexical signals, cross-language matching, and the integration of computational tools with scholarly interpretation. Many of these items, including systematic benchmarking, multi-channel detection, and rarity-aware scoring, remained unrealized.

This article reports on Tesserae V6, a platform that addresses several items on that agenda.[^1] The platform supports Greek and English texts alongside Latin. The fusion system described here was developed and evaluated first for Latin, where the most gold-standard benchmarks were available, and then extended to monolingual Greek, cross-lingual Greek-Latin, and experimental English cross-lingual search. The central contribution is *feature fusion*: the combination of independent search channels — lexical, phonetic, semantic, syntactic, and character-level similarity — into a unified detection and scoring framework. Rather than relying on any single signal of intertextual connection, fusion combines signals from channels that operate on fundamentally different linguistic representations. When multiple independent channels agree that a pair of passages constitutes a parallel, the convergence of evidence is stronger than any single channel's score alone.

The system employs ten scored channels spanning lexical, sub-lexical, distributional, and structural signals (described in "Platform Architecture and Search Channels"), combined through a weighted scoring layer that rewards cross-channel convergence while suppressing common-word noise (described in "Fusion Methodology").

When evaluated against five gold-standard benchmark datasets with parallels drawn from published scholarly commentaries, which include 862 parallel passages across five Latin text pairs, the fusion system achieves 91.9% recall, with individual benchmarks ranging from 89.2% to 96.2%. This represents a nearly fourfold improvement over the original Tesserae V3 baseline. The approximately 8% of parallels that remain undetected are predominantly thematic or conceptual and are so far uncaptured by Tesserae semantic channels. Benchmark-measured precision at rank 50 (P@50) is 22% on the largest benchmark (Lucan-Vergil), but this figure is a lower bound: the gold standards are compiled from published commentaries, which are themselves selective. Manual inspection of the top-50 results reveals that the majority of non-benchmark results are defensible intertextual connections — shared rare vocabulary, formulaic clausulae, synonym substitutions — that no commentator happened to catalog (see "Precision and Discovery" below).

The reference in the title of this article to "the Age of AI" reflects two distinct dimensions of artificial intelligence that run through this work. First, AI as a detection capability: one of the channels uses SPhilBERTa, a transformer-based language model trained on ancient texts, whose learned embeddings encode semantic relationships between words and sentences, enabling the detection of parallels between passages that share no vocabulary. Second, AI as a development tool: the platform was built through an extended collaboration between a humanities scholar and AI coding assistants (see "Development Methodology"), demonstrating that domain experts can direct AI tools to produce sophisticated computational systems when coupled with rigorous empirical validation.

The remainder of this article proceeds as follows. "From Lexical Matching to Internumerality" reviews the state of computational intertext detection since 2018. "Development Methodology" describes the AI-assisted development process that produced the platform. "Platform Architecture and Search Channels" presents the technical architecture, including the search channels organized by a formal taxonomy. "Fusion Methodology" describes in detail the core technical contribution of the scoring system. "Evaluation" presents results against five Latin benchmarks, case studies demonstrating multi-channel detection on Latin examples, Greek evaluation against one benchmark, a demonstration of English intra-language intertextual matching, and cross-lingual evaluation against Greek-Latin and English cross-lingual benchmarks. "Discussion and Conclusion" addresses implications, limitations, and future directions. Throughout, the article takes Latin as the primary example, since it is the most extensively resourced and studied case in terms of both NLP tools and gold-standard benchmarks, but Greek and English capabilities are evaluated in parallel and discussed on equal terms.

[^1]: The version numbering reflects an uneven development history. Version 4 was an ultimately unsuccessful attempt using an unorthodox set of languages and tools. Version 5 was essentially a translation of Version 3 from Perl to Python, with other platform modernizations; its core search functionality remained the V3 lemma-matching approach. Version 6 retains V3's lemma matching as one channel among ten.

[^2]: Tesserae V6 is under active development. The specific result positions reported in this article reflect the system's state at the time of testing (March 2026) and may shift as scoring parameters and channels are refined.

[^4]: See "The Limits of Lexical Detection" below for a detailed case study of this passage and its role in driving structural additions to the system.

## From Lexical Matching to Internumerality

For the purposes of intertext detection, lexical matching has proven effective. The original Tesserae studies demonstrated that the system could identify significant numbers of known allusions, while also finding previously unnoticed parallels that withstood scholarly scrutiny (Coffee et al. 2012; Forstall et al. 2015). Subsequent work applied the methodology to a range of author pairs and genres, and Burns (2017) used Tesserae to measure and map intergeneric allusion from Latin love elegy to Lucan's epic, finding substantially more intertextual connections than existing scholarship had identified.

Pure lexical matching has also shown its limits. Certain kinds of allusions, known since antiquity, were invisible to lemma matching. Thomas (1986, 179–180), following Macrobius (*Saturnalia* 6.2.13), demonstrates the range of non-lexical means by which Vergil's plague narrative recalls Lucretius, including an imitation in which, as Thomas (180) observes of *Georgics* 3.481 and *DRN* 6.1140, "not a single word is shared between the two lines" yet "the reminiscence is incontrovertible."[^4] Hinds (2020), while acknowledging the considerable advantages of digital lexical matching, argued for attention to dimensions of allusion that escape word-by-word concordance.

Several projects have made efforts to move computational intertext detection beyond purely lexical signals. Büchler et al. (2014) approached the problem of text reuse detection through multiple feature types, including string similarity, phonetic features, and semantic relationships, developing a pipeline for historical texts that anticipated the multi-channel approach. Chaudhuri and Dexter (2017) applied sequence alignment techniques drawn from genomics to detect inexact verbal echoes in Latin, demonstrating that character-level similarity methods could identify phonetic and morphological connections across large corpora that escape lemma-based matching. Forstall and Scheirer (2019), in *Quantitative Intertextuality*, provided the theoretical framework for this direction. They propose that "humans participate in semiotic systems as if they were solving a general-purpose pattern recognition problem" (18): readers embedded within a culture build internal models of that culture in stylistic terms, and "the reader's subjective experience in consuming a new work will derive in large part from the fulfillment or repudiation of expectations based on that internal model" (18). By laying out this analogous relationship, Forstall and Scheirer explain how textual data can be organized to make it computationally tractable. Crucially, they also argue in chapter 7 that effective intertext detection requires combining heterogeneous evidence types using "a principled information fusion strategy," which they call "meta-matching," using supervised machine learning to weight contributions from multiple feature types based on training data.

Manjavacas, Long, and Kestemont (2019) provided an important proof of concept for one strand of this program — and the closest prior work to V6's multi-channel concept — comparing lexical models, semantic models, and hybrid lexical-semantic models on a Latin text reuse detection task. While purely embedding-based approaches did not outperform lexical methods, hybrid approaches that combined both achieved the best results, suggesting that embeddings should supplement rather than replace traditional features. Burns et al. (2021) extended this work by systematically benchmarking word embedding models against a dataset of intertexts from Valerius Flaccus' *Argonautica*. Their approach successfully recovered parallels containing no overlapping words, such as *Phlegethontis operti* ("hidden Phlegethon," Valerius Flaccus, *Arg.* 1.735) and *Acherontis aperti* ("open Acheron," Statius, *Theb.* 11.150). Embedding models identify this non-lexical echo through semantic similarity between the river names and the antonymic adjectives. The development of domain-specific language models has further enhanced these capabilities. Riemenschneider and Frank (2023) developed SPhilBERTa, a trilingual sentence-RoBERTa model capable of detecting semantic similarities across Latin, Ancient Greek, and English. This cross-lingual capability is significant for classical studies, where the dialogue between Greek and Latin literature is foundational but has largely eluded computational formalization (Hinds 2020). Schelb et al. (2026), benchmarking retrieval models on a 545-pair Latin intertextuality dataset (Jerome and Lactantius against classical authors), found that the general multilingual E5-large model outperformed SPhilBERTa on passage retrieval (61% vs. 47% Recall@10), suggesting that domain-specific training does not always confer an advantage for retrieval tasks; their full two-stage pipeline (E5-large retriever plus XLM-RoBERTa cross-encoder reranker) achieved 79% Recall@100 with a 99% reduction in candidate space, validating a retrieve-and-rerank architecture complementary to V6's multi-channel fusion.

These studies, conducted on different benchmarks using different evaluation protocols, resist direct numerical comparison. Benchmark sizes range from dozens to hundreds of gold-standard pairs. Evaluation metrics vary: precision at rank K (P@K) measures what fraction of the top K results are true positives, recall at rank K (R@K) measures what fraction of gold-standard pairs appear within the top K results, and mean reciprocal rank (MRR) averages 1/rank for the first correct result across queries. The distinction between unsupervised detection (searching an entire text for any parallel) and query-based retrieval (ranking candidates for a pre-specified passage) fundamentally affects what the numbers mean. Nevertheless, a consistent pattern emerges when the available results are set side by side:

| Study | Benchmark | Approach | Recall metric |
|-------|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Coffee et al. 2012 | Lucan–Vergil (213 pairs) | Lexical (2+ shared lemmas) | ~27% |
| Manjavacas et al. 2019 | Bernard–Vulgate (Latin) | Semantic (TfIdf embeddings) | P@20 ≈ 31% |
| Manjavacas et al. 2019 | Bernard–Vulgate | Hybrid (soft cosine + embeddings) | P@20 ≈ 48% |
| Burns et al. 2021 | VF *Argonautica* 1 (945 pairs) | Semantic (word2vec bigrams) | Recall@250 ≈ 82%[^3] |
| Schelb et al. 2026 | Jerome/Lactantius (545 pairs) | E5-large + XLM-RoBERTa reranker | Recall@100 ≈ 79% |

[^3]: Query-based retrieval with pre-specified bigrams, not unsupervised detection; the high recall at K=250 reflects partly the advantage of knowing what to look for.

[^5]: V6 also differs from V3 in several infrastructure respects beyond the addition of new channels. Lemmatization uses a two-tier system with UD treebank lookup tables as primary and CLTK as fallback, rather than V3's dictionary-only approach, providing broader coverage of inflected forms. The inverted index has been rebuilt for the expanded corpus (1,429 Latin texts versus V3's smaller set). Latin orthographic normalization handles v/u, i/j, and enclitic separation (*-que*, *-ne*, *-ue*) systematically. The scoring algorithm preserves V3's IDF-plus-distance formula as the per-channel baseline, adding the multi-layer fusion scoring described in "Fusion Methodology."

Two patterns stand out. First, purely semantic approaches — whether using word2vec, FastText, or transformer-based models — consistently achieve 20–35% recall when applied as the sole detection signal in unsupervised settings. This ceiling appears to be a property of embedding-based similarity itself: the models capture broad thematic affinity but cannot reliably discriminate a true parallel from the many thematically related but non-parallel lines in a text. Second, hybrid approaches that combine semantic and lexical signals consistently outperform either alone (Manjavacas et al.'s soft cosine, Burns et al.'s query-based retrieval, Schelb et al.'s two-stage retrieve-and-rerank pipeline), confirming the principle that independent channels capture complementary dimensions of the intertextual signal.

Gong, Gero, and Schiefsky (2025) offer a complementary perspective with Intertext.AI, a reading interface that uses Latin BERT word-level contextual embeddings to identify potential intertextual connections. Tested on 56 lines of Lucan's *Bellum Civile* Book 1, their system detected 81.65% of commentary-attested references in its top-30 ranked results. The small sample and interactive evaluation protocol differ substantially from the corpus-scale benchmarks above, but their work demonstrates the value of contextual (as opposed to static) embeddings and of integrating computational detection into the scholarly reading process. Burns (forthcoming) traces the broader trajectory of this work as a shift from "intertextuality" to "internumerality": the use of mathematical representations to formalize and operationalize literary relationships.

Embedding-based semantics, non-lexical features, information fusion, and improved interfaces all converge in the design of Tesserae V6. The platform extends the hybrid approach pioneered by Manjavacas et al. (2019) from two signal types (lexical and semantic) to ten channels spanning four levels of linguistic representation, and from unsupervised similarity to a weighted fusion framework with convergence bonuses and rarity-aware scoring. It does not advance any single thread to its theoretical limit. Rather, it combines contributions from each into an integrated system that can be evaluated against scholarly standards and used in practice.

## Development Methodology: AI-Assisted Coding in Digital Humanities

Tesserae V6 was developed both to bring to users forms of intertextual search and analysis with consistently higher recall and better precision. This work was carried out through an extended collaboration between a humanities scholar (the project PI) and AI coding assistants, with no traditional programming by the researcher. This mode of development, sometimes called "vibecoding," represents a departure from traditional software engineering. Rather than writing code directly, the PI described desired functionality in natural language, reviewed outputs, and iteratively refined requirements based on observed behavior. The initial twelve-day development sprint in January 2026 used Replit Agent to produce the core platform. Intermediate development used Cursor, an AI-augmented code editor. Subsequent refinement, including fusion search design, scoring optimization, evaluation infrastructure, and the work reported in this article, was carried out over the following months using Claude Code (Anthropic). The PI directed development by articulating feature requirements, evaluating outputs against scholarly standards, identifying edge cases and failure modes, and providing domain knowledge about Latin and Greek linguistics. The AI assistants translated these specifications into working code in Python and React, making architectural and implementation decisions that the PI could then evaluate.

This development mode revealed characteristic failure patterns. Context window limitations meant that AI assistants operating within a limited memory horizon could make changes to one component while forgetting relevant constraints in another, producing subtle inconsistencies. It was this problem, together with considerations of cost, that drove a progression through three AI platforms. Initial development used Replit Agent running on Replit's cloud servers, which offered rapid prototyping but had a small context window and was expensive. The shift to Cursor, an AI-augmented code editor running on a university server, reduced costs but provided a base AI that lacked the breadth of vision needed for coordinated multi-component changes. The final shift to Claude Code on the same university server eliminated the PI's server costs entirely and provided an agent with substantially superior planning and context management capabilities. Domain knowledge gaps led to issues that a classicist would catch immediately. For example, early versions treated Latin orthographic variants (v/u, i/j) as distinct characters, failing to recognize that "vada" and "uada" represent the same word. Framework migrations could silently break API connections. When the frontend migrated from vanilla JavaScript to React, the assistant could not always retain full awareness of which components had been updated.

In addition to migrating across agents, several practices emerged as strategies to mitigate the work process challenges. A persistent documentation file (`CLAUDE.md`) served as memory across conversation sessions, recording architectural decisions, known issues, and constraints. Reference searches that ensured "arma virum" returned expected matches from Ovid and Quintilian prevented regressions during refactoring. Incremental development, implementing minimal versions and iterating based on observed behavior, proved more productive than specifying complete feature sets upfront.

A natural question about AI-assisted development is whether the resulting software can be trusted. The answer lies not in any single safeguard but in a layered validation strategy that operated throughout development. The system is built from well-understood, standard components. Text data enters as `.tess` files with standardized formatting. NLP processing uses established packages: CLTK for Latin lemmatization, Stanza/LatinPipe for dependency parsing, SPhilBERTa for semantic embeddings. The core matching algorithms (Levenshtein distance, cosine similarity, Jaccard index, inverse document frequency) are standard information retrieval techniques with known properties. These are verifiable building blocks, not opaque systems. For each major component (matching, scoring, and fusion) the PI reviewed explanations of code logic from the AI assistants. The AI described what the code does; the PI evaluated whether what it does is correct given domain knowledge of Latin linguistics, scholarly practice, and the intended behavior. This is analogous to code review in traditional software engineering, with the difference that the reviewer evaluates logic rather than syntax.

The system was also evaluated continuously against five gold-standard benchmark datasets comprising 862 parallel passages drawn from published scholarly commentaries (described in detail in "Benchmarks" under "Evaluation"). Benchmarking was not a post-hoc validation step but inherent in the iterative development. Each change to matching, scoring, or fusion logic was tested against all five benchmarks before being retained. A change that improved recall on one benchmark while degrading another was investigated and revised. This cycle of implementing, measuring, diagnosing, and revising operated across hundreds of development iterations. Subject-matter experts also reviewed output with responses that led to major improvements.

For example, the parallel between Silius Italicus, *Punica* 2.536 (*quis Acheronta moues*) and Vergil, *Aeneid* 7.312 (*Acheronta mouebo*), an allusion involving the rare proper noun Acheron, initially ranked at #1898 because the scoring system rewarded quantity of shared words over rarity. Two common function words outscored one rare proper noun. Expert identification of this problem triggered a multi-phase scoring redesign: geometric mean IDF normalization, headword frequency correction, min-IDF convergence weighting, and stoplist-based function-word detection (see "Common-Word Suppression"). The result promoted the parallel to rank #14 and suppressed common-word noise from the top ranks across all benchmarks. Beyond benchmarks, targeted case-study testing took specific scholarly arguments about difficult parallels — Vergil's non-lexical imitation of Lucretius noted above (see "The Limits of Lexical Detection"), Hinds (2020) on Greek-Latin epic incipits (see "Cross-Lingual Detection") — and used them as diagnostic probes, running the cited passages through the system, identifying why particular parallels were missed, and implementing fixes validated against the full benchmark suite.

The collaboration produced a sophisticated platform that would have been beyond either party's individual capacity, since the PI lacked programming skills and the AI lacked deep understanding of classical intertextuality. This suggests a model for future digital humanities projects where researchers with specialized domain knowledge but limited technical training can develop custom computational tools. At the same time, the characteristic failure modes require attention. Context limitations and migration issues are not insurmountable, but they demand new practices for documentation, testing, and knowledge transfer that differ from traditional software engineering workflows.

## Platform Architecture and Search Channels

Tesserae V6 is implemented as a web application with a Python 3.11 Flask backend and a React 18 frontend built with Vite and Tailwind CSS. It is deployed on a University at Buffalo server using Apache with mod_wsgi. The corpus contains over 2,100 texts in Latin, Greek, and English. The Latin collection alone comprises 1,429 texts spanning 528,000 lines of poetry and prose, from Ennius to the nineteenth century, predominantly classical and late antique but including Carolingian, medieval, and Neo-Latin works. Text processing uses lookup tables derived from Universal Dependencies treebanks (approximately 62,000 Latin lemma mappings, approximately 58,000 Greek) with CLTK as a fallback lemmatizer. A pre-built inverted index (~2.4 GB for Latin) enables sub-second lookup of all corpus lines containing any given lemma.

The search channels are classified into four types based on the level of linguistic representation at which they operate. This classification is not merely descriptive; it determines each channel's behavior under textual unit variation and governs the design of the two-pass search architecture (see "Two-Pass Line/Window Architecture"). The four lexical channels match on the identity of word forms or dictionary headwords within a textual unit. They require matching tokens to co-occur within a single unit: when an allusion is split across a line break (enjambment), neither line alone meets the threshold, but combining them into a two-line window recovers the match.

| Channel | Signal | Criterion |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| lemma (2-word) | Shared dictionary headwords | ≥ 2 shared lemmata after stoplist filtering |
| lemma (1-word) | Single shared headword | ≥ 1 shared lemma (high-recall variant) |
| exact | Identical surface tokens | ≥ 2 shared orthographic forms |
| rare_word | Shared low-frequency lemmata | ≥ 2 shared lemmata with corpus frequency ≤ 50 |

The standard two-word lemma channel is the classic Tesserae approach, inherited from Version 3. The single-word variant was the single highest-impact addition in the fusion study, recovering allusions built around a single pivotal term. Most famously, Lucan's *Bella... canimus* ("Wars... we sing," *Bellum Civile* 1.2) echoing Vergil's *Arma... cano* ("Arms... I sing," *Aeneid* 1.1), linked by the single shared lemma *cano*. Over half of the parallels missed by two-word matching share exactly one rare content word. The rare word channel targets lemmas appearing in 50 or fewer texts across the corpus. This channel instantiates the scholarly observation that when two passages share such an uncommon word, the probability of coincidence decreases substantially.

Two sub-lexical channels match on character-level similarity between individual token pairs, comparing every source token against every target token.

| Channel | Signal | Criterion |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| edit_distance | Levenshtein distance | ≥ 60% normalized similarity |
| sound | Character trigram overlap | ≥ 25% Jaccard similarity |

These channels detect morphological and phonetic echoes invisible to lemmatization. The edit distance channel catches cases like *ferrea belligeri* (Lucan 1.62) echoing *belli ferratos* (Vergil, *Aeneid* 7.622), where morphologically related words (*ferrea/ferratos*, *belligeri/belli*) share roots but have different dictionary headwords. The sound channel detects phonetic texture. Lines heavy with similar character sequences score highly against each other, identifying alliterative and assonant echoes.

Two distributional channels match on semantic relatedness between individual tokens — "distributional" in the sense that they represent word meaning through patterns of co-occurrence, whether learned from large corpora (embeddings) or curated from lexicographic resources (synonym dictionaries).

| Channel | Signal | Criterion |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| semantic | Neural embedding similarity | SPhilBERTa cosine similarity ≥ 0.5 |
| dictionary | Curated synonym pairs | ≥ 2 shared synonym pairs from V3 database (23,833 entries) |

The semantic channel uses SPhilBERTa (Riemenschneider and Frank 2023), a sentence transformer trained on parallel Latin-Greek texts, to encode each line as a 768-dimensional vector. Lines expressing similar ideas receive similar representations even when they share no vocabulary, enabling detection of purely conceptual parallels. The dictionary channel draws on curated Latin synonym pairs from Tesserae V3's synonymy database, derived from the Lewis and Short lexicon, to detect deliberate synonym substitution, as when a later poet writes *mentes* where the model had *animos*, or *pontus* where the original had *mare*.

A single structural channel matches on syntactic patterns derived from dependency parses.

| Channel | Signal | Criterion |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| syntax | Dependency-tree pattern overlap | Shared UD dependency triples |

The syntax channel uses pre-computed analyses from LatinPipe, with its 90–95% Labeled Attachment Score. Parsing the complete Latin corpus (542,311 lines, 1,429 texts) required approximately 16 hours and produced a 1.6 GB database of per-line annotations including tokens, lemmas, universal part-of-speech tags, dependency heads, relation labels, and morphological features. This "parse once, query forever" architecture means the expensive computational step need not be repeated, and the syntax data is accessible to tools beyond Tesserae itself. Two passages may echo each other through parallel grammatical construction (both building genitive-noun chains, both placing a verb between two accusative objects) even when they share no vocabulary. The syntax channel compares these dependency patterns, addressing structural echoes without shared words or, in practice with fusion, boosting scores of lexical matches with a high degree of syntactic similarity.[^5]

Beyond fusion, V6 introduces several features not available in earlier versions. A *rare word search* identifies shared hapax legomena or low-frequency terms across texts. A *rare pair search* detects shared bigrams of uncommon words. A *line search* allows users to search for parallels to a single specified line rather than comparing full text pairs. A *corpus-wide search* extends any discovered parallel across the entire Latin corpus, retrieving all other passages that match the same signal profile. And a *cross-language search* enables comparison across Greek, Latin, and English, leveraging SPhilBERTa's multilingual embeddings and bilingual dictionaries extracted from the Perseus Digital Library to identify parallels across language boundaries (see "Cross-Lingual Detection" below). V6 also implements a collaborative intertext repository where scholars can save discovered parallels with structured metadata and interpretive annotations attributed via ORCID, realizing a goal articulated in Coffee (2018) for a shared, searchable database of attested intertexts. These features are described in the platform's online documentation and may be treated in detail elsewhere; this article focuses on the fusion system as V6's central methodological contribution.

## Fusion Methodology

This section describes the technical core of the article: the fusion scoring system that combines ten scored channels into a single ranked output. The design reflects an iterative process of development, evaluation, and refinement, with each phase responding to problems identified in the previous one.

### Weighted Score Fusion

The scoring system described below is applied identically to both the line pass and the window pass; each pass produces its own fused ranking, and the two are merged with deduplication (see "Two-Pass Line/Window Architecture" below). This section describes the core scoring mechanism.

Each channel independently scores its matches using Tesserae's V3 scoring function, inverse document frequency weighted by token distance. The fusion layer combines these per-channel scores into a single fused score using a rarity-weighted formula:

> fused_score = m² × Σ_{i=1}^{C} (score_i × w_i)  +  m × β × max(0, N_w − 1)

In plain terms: each channel that detects a match contributes its score multiplied by the channel's weight. These weighted scores are summed to produce a base score. The base score is then scaled by a rarity multiplier that reflects how distinctive the matched vocabulary is — common words are heavily penalized, rare words are boosted. On top of this, a convergence bonus rewards pairs detected by multiple channels, since independent agreement across different linguistic representations is stronger evidence of a genuine parallel than a high score from any single channel. The convergence bonus is itself modulated by word rarity, preventing common function words from accumulating undeserved credit simply because they trigger many channels.

Formally: *C* is the number of channels that found the pair, *score_i* is channel *i*'s V3 score, *w_i* is the channel weight, *m* is the rarity multiplier (a piecewise function of the geometric mean corpus IDF across matched words; see Rarity Scoring below), *N_w* = *N* × min(1, IDF_min)² is the IDF-weighted channel count (see Convergence Bonus below), and β = 0.75 is the convergence bonus coefficient.

Channel weights were determined by systematic grid search across approximately 35,000 parameter configurations on five benchmark pairs, optimizing for recall at varying rank thresholds (recall@100, recall@500, recall@1000). Each weight reflects the channel's empirical contribution to recovering gold-standard parallels:

| Channel | Weight | Type | Rationale |
|---------|--------|------|-----------|
| sound | 4.0 | sub-lexical | Phonetic allusions are a distinct strategy; high precision when detected |
| edit_distance | 2.0 | sub-lexical | Morphological echoes invisible to lemmatization |
| lemma (2-word) | 2.0 | lexical | Workhorse channel; moderate weight avoids dominance |
| rare_word | 2.0 | lexical | Shared uncommon vocabulary is strong evidence |
| semantic | 1.2 | distributional | Broad coverage, moderate precision per pair |
| exact | 1.0 | lexical | Verbatim quotation is strong evidence but largely redundant with lemma |
| dictionary | 0.5 | distributional | Curated synonyms capture deliberate substitution |
| syntax | 0.3 | structural | Currently limited to pre-computed treebanks |
| syntax (structural) | 0.5 | structural | Identical dependency head patterns without shared lemmas (see Georgics case study below); gated by dictionary confirmation or high cosine (≥ 0.70) to suppress false positives from common syntactic patterns |
| lemma (1-word) | 0.3 | lexical | High recall, low precision; downweighted accordingly |

The weight optimization used a two-phase approach to keep the search space tractable. Phase one swept approximately 35,000 weight combinations with fixed convergence and penalty parameters; phase two swept 25 convergence-penalty combinations using the best weights from phase one. The entire process, including channel caching and grid sweep, completed in under 20 minutes using numpy-vectorized scoring on cached channel results.

### Convergence Bonus

On top of the weighted sum, a convergence bonus of 0.75 per additional confirming channel rewards cross-channel agreement. When multiple independent signals, each operating on a different linguistic representation, flag the same pair of lines, this is stronger evidence of a genuine parallel than a high score from any single channel. A pair found by seven channels receives a convergence bonus of +4.5, while a pair found by only two receives +0.75.

The convergence bonus is modulated by word rarity: the channel count is multiplied by min(1.0, min_word_idf)², where min_word_idf is the corpus IDF of the least rare matched word. This continuous Zipf-like scaling means that a pair whose weakest word has IDF ~0.3 (a common word) gets only 9% of the full convergence credit, while a pair where all words have IDF above 1.0 gets full credit. Crucially, the minimum word IDF is used rather than the geometric mean: "nec absistit" is gated by *nec*'s low IDF (0.444), not rescued by *absisto*'s high IDF (2.65). This prevents a rare partner word from masking a common function word.

Convergence is zeroed entirely for three categories: (1) single-word matches, where one shared word detected by multiple channels does not constitute genuine convergence; (2) all-function-word matches; and (3) mixed function-plus-content-word matches, where the function word inflates the channel count without adding allusion signal.

### Rarity Scoring

The fused score is scaled by a rarity multiplier derived from the geometric mean corpus IDF of the matched words. The multiplier follows a piecewise curve: near-stopwords (geometric mean IDF < 0.1) receive a flat floor penalty of 0.05; common words (0.1–1.5) follow a linear ramp from 0.15 to 1.0; rare words (> 1.5) receive a logarithmic boost capped at 2.0. The multiplier is squared before application to the base score, making the penalty steep: a pair with multiplier 0.5 retains only 25% of its base score, while a pair with multiplier 0.05 retains only 0.25%.

This three-layer system (base multiplier, convergence weighting, and rarity boost) was developed iteratively to solve the problem of common-word matches dominating the top results. The intellectual argument is straightforward: when two Latin poetic lines share only *nec* and *tum*, this is grammatical co-occurrence, not allusion. Even if six channels independently detect the match, because *nec* appears frequently enough to trigger lexical, exact, and edit distance channels, the convergence provides no additional evidence of literary engagement. The rarity-aware scoring formalizes this intuition.

### Common-Word Suppression

The most technically challenging aspect of the scoring system was suppressing common-word matches without collateral damage to legitimate parallels.

After initial weight optimization, common Latin function words such as *nec*, *tum*, *inde*, *quam*, and *per* persisted in the top 50 results. Root causes included an asymmetric-pair escape path (a rare word paired with a common word produces a moderate geometric mean IDF that escapes threshold-based filtering) and a lemmatization gap (the inverted index stores inflected forms separately, so "quem" with df=164 looks rare while its headword "qui" with df=1426 is extremely common). The lemmatization gap was addressed through headword IDF normalization: for each lemma in the index, its corpus document frequency is replaced by the maximum of its own frequency and that of its dictionary headword. This ensures that "quem," "quos," and "quibus" all inherit the high frequency of "qui." The asymmetric-pair problem was addressed by switching the convergence IDF weight from geometric mean to minimum word IDF. With the geometric mean, a pair like "nec + absisto" (IDFs 0.444 and 2.65) produces a moderate value (~1.08) that escapes binary thresholds. With the minimum, the pair is gated by *nec*'s low IDF, receiving only 20% of the full convergence credit.

However, parameters tuned on one text pair (Aeneid 7 versus Silius Italicus' *Punica* 2, characterized by rare mythological vocabulary) proved catastrophic for a different text pair (Aeneid 1 versus Lucan's *Bellum Civile* 1, characterized by common epic vocabulary like *pectus*, *cura*, *arma*, *fatum* with corpus IDFs of 0.35–0.50, overlapping with function words like *nec* at 0.44). This cross-text-pair instability revealed that IDF-based thresholds cannot cleanly separate function words from common content words, because their frequency ranges overlap.

The solution was to replace IDF-based function-word detection entirely with a curated stoplist of 90 Latin function words (pronouns, conjunctions, prepositions, common adverbs, and high-frequency verbs) already defined in the system's matching layer. The stoplist provides the precision that frequency alone cannot: the word *tum* ("then," IDF ~0.3) and the word *pectore* ("in the breast," IDF ~0.4) have similar corpus frequencies, but only the first is a function word. Three penalty tiers based on stoplist classification:

| Match type | Condition | Penalty | Convergence |
|------------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| All content words | All matched words are content words | IDF curve only | Full (IDF-weighted) |
| Mixed (function + content) | At least one function word, at least one content word | Single-word penalty (0.12) | Zeroed |
| All function words | All matched words are function words | Heavy penalty (0.50) | Zeroed |
| Single word only | Only one unique word matched | Single-word penalty (0.12) | Zeroed |

Importantly, individual channels run without stoplist filtering. They cast the widest possible net. The stoplist is applied only in the scoring layer, shaping the ranking without reducing recall. The result of this iterative refinement was that zero function words remained in the top 50 results of the standard Lucan *Bellum Civile* 1 - *Aeneid* test, while legitimate common-vocabulary allusions like "pectore curas" (*Aeneid* 1.227 and *Bellum Civile* 1.272) remain at rank 46, suppressed relative to rare-vocabulary parallels but not buried. The 5-benchmark recall remained unchanged at 91.9% throughout all phases of common-word suppression.

All IDF values described above are computed by default across the full Latin corpus (1,605 texts). However, V6 also offers a meter-specific frequency baseline as an advanced option (see "Precision and Discovery" below for the rationale). When enabled, IDF is computed only across texts sharing the same metrical tradition — for example, the 214 hexameter texts. This compresses the dynamic range of rarity scores (since vocabulary common within a meter is no longer inflated by its rarity across prose, lyric, and elegiac texts) and requires a correspondingly tighter single-word penalty to maintain separation between multi-word and single-word matches. The system adjusts this automatically.

### Two-Pass Line/Window Architecture

Classical verse is composed in lines, but allusions frequently span line breaks. In Latin hexameter poetry, enjambment is a deliberate stylistic choice, and the shared vocabulary of an allusion may be distributed across two or more consecutive lines. A system that operates only on individual lines will systematically miss these cases.

The channel taxonomy described in "Platform Architecture and Search Channels" provides a principled basis for addressing this through selective windowing. In the first pass, all ten channels run on individual verse lines, providing complete coverage of every matching signal. In the second pass, only channels whose matching criterion requires co-occurrence of multiple features within a textual unit run on two-line windows. The window pass runs the three lexical channels (lemma, single-lemma, rare_word) and the dictionary channel. Sub-lexical and distributional channels are excluded because they measure pairwise relationships between individual tokens already exhaustively enumerated in the line pass; expanding the unit does not introduce new token comparisons. Profiling confirmed this analysis: on *Georgics* versus *Bellum Civile*, the excluded channels consumed 84% of window-pass time while producing effectively zero novel matches. Line and window results are merged with deduplication: line results take priority, and a window result is included only if it covers at least one source-target line pair not already present.

#### Enjambment Quality Filter

Not all window results represent genuine enjambment. A two-line window may match simply because both lines independently contain common vocabulary, with the matched words falling entirely on one line while the other line contributes nothing. To distinguish genuine cross-line allusions from these false positives, a post-fusion filter classifies each window result into one of three categories based on the positions of matched words relative to the line boundary:

1. Genuine enjambment. Matched words appear on both lines of at least one side (source or target). The result is kept as a two-line display, but its score is decayed by a cross-break gap penalty:

> penalty = exp(−α × gap)

where *gap* is the number of tokens between the last matched word before the line break and the first matched word after it (on the side with the widest gap), and α = 0.25. This rewards tight enjambment — matched words close to the break, as in a noun on line 1 and its adjective modifier run over to line 2 — while penalizing window results where matched words happen to fall far apart on different lines. A gap of zero (adjacent tokens across the break) receives no penalty; a gap of 4 tokens reduces the score to 37%; a gap of 10 tokens reduces it to 8%.

2. False enjambment. Matched words fall entirely on one line of both source and target windows. The result is trimmed to display only the line containing the matches, preserving the match in the ranked output as a single-line result rather than discarding it.

3. Fallback. If matched-word positions cannot be resolved (e.g., when a channel stores lemma forms rather than surface forms), the result is kept as a two-line window display with no gap penalty applied. This is conservative: the match is retained in the output rather than discarded, at the cost of occasionally displaying two lines when one would suffice.

#### Window Contribution to Recall

Across five evaluation benchmarks, the sliding window raises overall recall from 84.6% (line-only) to 91.9% (line plus window), recovering 63 enjambed allusions at minimal computational cost:

| Benchmark | Line-only | Line + Window | Δ |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|---|
| Lucan–Vergil | 170/213 (79.8%) | 190/213 (89.2%) | +20 |
| VF–Vergil | 447/521 (85.8%) | 481/521 (92.3%) | +34 |
| Achilleid–Vergil | 46/53 (86.8%) | 50/53 (94.3%) | +4 |
| Achilleid–Ovid | 19/23 (82.6%) | 21/23 (91.3%) | +2 |
| Achilleid–Thebaid | 47/52 (90.4%) | 50/52 (96.2%) | +3 |
| Total | 729/862 (84.6%) | 792/862 (91.9%) | +63 |

The window recall figures reflect a deduplication step that removes visually redundant results from overlapping windows. Because 2-line sliding windows overlap by one line, the same match can appear up to four times in different source×target window combinations. The system deduplicates by retaining only the highest-scoring window when overlapping windows match the same set of lemmas. This eliminates visual clutter in the ranked output at a small theoretical cost: six gold-standard pairs found only by lower-scoring overlapping windows at extreme ranks (beyond position 198,000 of ~300,000 total results) are lost. At any practical rank cutoff, recall is identical to the non-deduplicated version.

### Design Principles

Several principles guided the scoring design. First, channels cast the widest possible net, and the scoring layer handles ranking; this separation ensures that no potential parallel is missed, only downranked. Second, in fusion mode, per-channel "boosts" (phonetic similarity, edit distance) are disabled to prevent double-counting. Each channel scores purely on its own signal, and the fusion weights handle all inter-channel combination. Third, each channel retains at most its top 50,000 scored results before fusion, a standard rank-based pruning step in multi-source fusion pipelines, since results below this threshold have negligible marginal contribution to fused scores. The semantic channel applies a tighter per-source-line cap: only the top 100 target lines by cosine similarity are retained for each source line, filtering out the long tail of marginally similar pairs. These per-channel caps affect only the inputs to the fusion layer; the final fused ranking is uncapped, so benchmark evaluation counts a gold pair as found at whatever rank it ultimately achieves. Finally, parameters must generalize across text pairs with different vocabulary profiles, from rare mythological vocabulary to common epic vocabulary to dense allusive practice. The five-benchmark recall test is necessary but not sufficient; we verified each parameter configuration against additional text pairs beyond the five benchmarks to confirm that improvements were not overfitting to the benchmark vocabulary profiles.

A system result matches a gold pair when source references match exactly and target references match within ±3 lines.

Benchmark datasets are the most rigorous available method for evaluating intertext detection, but they are approximations of a phenomenon that is ultimately subjective. Whether two passages are "related" depends on what counts as a relationship, and scholars can have different approaches and emphases that shape the resulting dataset. The Lucan-Vergil benchmark, by design, is enriched for parallels that share vocabulary (its types 4–5 gold standard requires "identifiable lexical or phrasal connection"). The VF-Vergil benchmark takes a more inclusive approach, recording all parallels attested by any commentary with no strength typology, producing a large dataset that includes everything from near-quotation to loose thematic affinity. The Achilleid benchmarks occupy a middle ground: the two-commentator consensus requirement filters for reliability but does not specify what kind of evidence underlies that consensus — the annotations include verbal echoes alongside contextual notes like "même aposiopèse" (same rhetorical break-off) and "contexte semblable" (similar context). The Knauer Iliad-Aeneid benchmark (1964), discussed in detail below, is the most comprehensive, cataloging every connection Knauer observed from near-translations to narrative-structural parallels with no shared vocabulary. The Hunter Apollonius-Homer benchmark (1989) uses a five-level typology similar to Lucan-Vergil but covers only the first third of *Argonautica* 3. These differences are not flaws but reflections of a genuinely multidimensional phenomenon, and they mean that recall and precision figures across benchmarks are not always directly comparable. The results that follow should be read in this context.

#### Channel Contribution Analysis

The multi-channel approach reveals dimensions of intertextual practice invisible to lexical matching alone, contributing  to the jump from 35.2% to 76.5%. Single-word allusions prove pervasive: the lemma_min1 channel, requiring only one shared lemma, added 27 unique gold pairs on the Lucan-Vergil benchmark alone, parallels where the later poet echoes a single pivotal word from the predecessor. Phonetic echoes constitute a distinct allusive strategy; the sound channel achieves P@10 of 30–60% across benchmarks, and when it fires, the result is often a genuine parallel. Morphological echoes are caught by edit distance: seven unique gold pairs on Lucan-Vergil were found only by this channel, cases where morphologically related words share roots but have different dictionary headwords. The dictionary channel confirms synonym substitution as a real practice: eight unique gold pairs were found only through curated synonym pairs, instances where a later poet deliberately substituted a synonym for a word in the model text. The SPhilBERTa-based semantic channel identifies parallels sharing zero vocabulary, such as Lucan 1.491 and Vergil, *Aeneid* 2.796 (cosine similarity 0.643), where both passages describe crowds in motion during an urban crisis. And the syntax channel, operating on pre-computed dependency parses, identifies parallel grammatical constructions even without shared vocabulary, as in Lucan 1.588 and Vergil, *Aeneid* 3.361, where both passages use augural language with similar genitive-noun chain structures. Notably, the channels' collective coverage is robust to individual channel quality: replacing SPhilBERTa with Schelb et al.'s fine-tuned E5-large model, which doubles standalone semantic retrieval performance (Recall@10: 18.3% vs. 8.5%), produced zero additional recall in the fusion pipeline, because every pair the semantic channel uniquely surfaces is already captured by one or more lexical channels. This redundancy is a feature, not a limitation — it means the architecture degrades gracefully and that no single channel is a bottleneck.

A direct comparison of the fusion system with V3-style lemma matching on Aeneid 7 versus Silius Italicus' *Punica* 2 reveals the difference in ranking quality. Of V3's top 227 results, only 15 (6.6%) appear in fusion's top 15, the genuine quality parallels (shared rare vocabulary like *serpens/hydra*, *aurum/trilix*). Meanwhile, 186 of 227 (81.9%) fall to fusion rank 5000 or below, with a median rank of 93,873. These are all common-word bigrams: *ipse/fero*, *per/urbs*, *ille/uolo*, *enim/neque*, that V3 incorrectly ranks as top results due to lower sensitivity to frequency.

These figures measure agreement between the system's top-ranked output and commentary-attested parallels. The gold-standard pairs are drawn from published scholarly commentaries, and they include the full range of allusive practice: direct quotation, synonym substitution, morphological echo, and formulaic reuse. A potential objection is that many highly ranked results are "merely formulaic" — shared phrases like *ad aethera clamor* ("shout to the heavens"), *pectore curas* ("cares in the breast"), and *occupat artus* ("seizes the limbs") that belong to the common stock of epic diction. But such pairs are in the benchmarks precisely because commentators judged them significant. The objection conflates two distinct questions: (1) whether a verbal echo exists between two passages, and (2) whether a poet intended that echo as an allusion. The computational system addresses question (1). Question (2) remains the province of the scholar. As Conte (1986) argued, the reuse of formulaic language in Roman poetry is itself a form of intertextual engagement: poets choose which formulae to adopt and where to deploy them, and these choices carry meaning. A system that identifies all instances of formula-sharing between two texts provides the raw material for studying these patterns of selective reuse.

An independent validation of the channel quality hierarchy was obtained by having a large language model (Claude Sonnet 4.6, Anthropic 2026) classify the top 200 results from each of the five benchmarks — 1,000 pairs total — as (A) strong allusion, (B) possible echo, (C) formulaic overlap, or (D) unlikely connection. Of the 1,000 pairs, 31% received A or B ratings. The LLM classifications correlated strongly with specific channels: shared rare vocabulary (the rare_word channel) was the strongest predictor of quality (1.94× lift for A/B classification), followed by phonetic similarity (sound, 1.74× lift). The dictionary channel, by contrast, slightly predicted noise (0.86× lift), suggesting that synonym matches alone — without supporting evidence from other channels — are more often coincidental than allusive. Channel convergence was the clearest quality signal: pairs detected by six or more independent channels received A/B classifications 57–71% of the time, compared to 20% for two-channel pairs. These findings guided a retuning of the fusion weights, increasing the contribution of the rare_word and sound channels and the convergence bonus while reducing the dictionary channel's influence. One persistent challenge is what might be called the "rare epithet" problem: fixed collocations like *Neptunia proles* ("Neptune's offspring," Messapus's standard epithet) are corpus-rare in IDF terms but genre-formulaic, recurring because they are metrically convenient rather than because of allusive intent. V6 addresses this through an alternative frequency baseline: users can switch from corpus-wide IDF (computed across all 1,605 Latin texts) to meter-specific IDF (computed across, e.g., the 214 hexameter texts in the corpus). Under hexameter IDF, *arma* appears in 89% of hexameter texts (versus 57% of the full corpus), so its rarity score drops sharply, while a genuinely distinctive word retains its premium. The meter grouping — hexameter, elegiac, lyric, dramatic, prose — reflects the observation that metrically convenient vocabulary recurs within the same metrical tradition. *Neptunia proles* fills a hexameter cadence and appears across epic, didactic, and panegyric; *me miserum*, by contrast, is an elegiac convention that would not be deflated by hexameter IDF. The system classifies each of the 1,609 Latin texts by era (drawn from existing author chronologies), meter (derived from scansion data and genre inference), and genre, all editable through the administrative interface. The default remains corpus-wide IDF, preserving backward compatibility; the meter-specific baseline is available as an advanced option for scholars who wish to foreground allusive distinctiveness over formulaic convention.

Moreover, the implicit assumption that non-gold results are false positives is misleading. Commentaries are themselves selective: each commentator identifies the parallels most relevant to their interpretive argument, not an exhaustive inventory. The Lucan-Vergil benchmark draws on four commentaries published between 1909 and 2009; the Valerius Flaccus benchmark on three. Manual inspection of the top 50 results on Lucan-Vergil reveals that 11 of 50 match gold-standard parallels (P@50 = 22%). The majority of the remaining 39 non-gold results are, on philological inspection, defensible intertextual connections involving shared rare vocabulary, formulaic clausulae, or synonym substitution. Two examples illustrate the range:

- Lucan 1.446 / Vergil, *Aeneid* 7.764: *Et Taranis Scythicae non mitior ara Dianae* / *litora, pinguis ubi et placabilis ara Dianae*. The shared clausula *ara Dianae* links two passages describing Diana's altar — Lucan's as "no gentler" than Scythian sacrifice, Vergil's as "placable" — a contrastive echo that no commentator in the benchmark has catalogued. Detected by six channels.

- Lucan 1.638 / Vergil, *Aeneid* 6.99: *Involvens multaque tegens ambage canebat* / *horrendas canit ambages antroque remugit*. Both passages describe prophetic utterance with the rare noun *ambages* ("riddling words") and the verb *cano* ("sing/chant"), in reversed word order — the prophetess at Lucan's *Bellum Civile* 1.638 and the Sibyl at *Aeneid* 6.99. Detected by four channels.

If even half of the 39 novel non-benchmark results are real parallels (and manual inspection suggests the proportion is substantially higher), then the true P@50 on Lucan-Vergil is closer to 50% or above rather than the benchmark-measured 22%. Benchmark P@K is a lower bound on actual precision.

#### Case Studies

##### Multi-Channel Convergence: "obstipuere animi"

The power of multi-channel fusion is most visible in cases where multiple independent signals converge on the same parallel. The parallel between Vergil, *Aeneid* 5.404 (*Obstipuere animi: tantorum ingentia septem*) and Statius, *Thebaid* 6.738 (*obstipuere animi, fecitque silentia terror*) is detected by nine channels simultaneously, ranking at position 11 in a book-level search (*Aeneid* 5 × *Thebaid* 6). The channels include exact token matching (*obstipuere*, *animi*), lemma matching (*obstipesco*, *animus*), dictionary (synonym pairs), semantic (conceptual similarity), and, notably, edit distance. The words *ingentia* (in Vergil's line) and *silentia* (in Statius's) share 75% character-level similarity (8-letter words both ending in *-entia*), above the 0.7 edit distance threshold. The sound channel detects the overlapping phonetic texture of these morphologically related endings. This example illustrates a type of allusion that no single channel could confidently identify but that the convergence of multiple independent signals elevates to near-certainty. The morphological echo between *ingentia* and *silentia*, words with no etymological relationship but striking phonetic parallelism, is precisely the kind of allusive effect that can be detected with sensitivity beyond exact word and lemma matching.

##### The Gates of War: Edit Distance and Morphological Echo

The parallel between Lucan 1.62 (*Ferrea belligeri compescat limina Iani*) and Vergil, *Aeneid* 7.622 (*belli ferratos rumpit Saturnia postes*) was found only by the edit distance channel. The morphological echoes *ferrea/ferratos* and *belligeri/belli* share roots but have different dictionary headwords: *ferreus* versus *ferratus*, and the compound *belliger* versus simple *bellum*. Lemma matching sees no connection; edit distance, measuring character-level similarity, detects the shared root morphemes. This is a known, important allusion in the scholarly literature, Lucan's reimagining of Vergil's Gates of War, that traditional computational methods could not detect.

##### Synonym Substitution: "mentes animosque"

Lucan 1.354 (*Quamquam caede feras mentes animosque tumentes*) and Vergil, *Aeneid* 1.202 (*revocate animos, maestumque timorem*) share the synonym pair *mens/animus* from the V3 dictionary database. Both lines also share the lemma *animus* (*animosque*/*animos*), so the lemma channel detects the pair as well. But the dictionary channel adds a connection that lemma matching cannot: the link between *mentes* (lemma *mens*) and *animos* (lemma *animus*) as synonyms. This parallel illustrates the Latin practice of *uariatio*, where a later poet deploys both a word and its synonym in the same line, enriching the echo. The *mens/animus* synonym link, invisible to lemma matching (the two words have different headwords), is captured by the curated synonym resource.

##### Conceptual Parallel Without Shared Vocabulary

Lucan 1.491 (*incerti, quo quemque fugae tulit impetus, urgent*, "uncertain, they press on wherever the impulse of flight has carried each one") and Vergil, *Aeneid* 2.796 (*atque hic ingentem comitum adfluxisse novorum*, "and here a great throng of new companions had streamed in") share zero vocabulary, yet the SPhilBERTa semantic channel detects a cosine similarity of 0.643. Both passages describe crowds in panicked motion during an urban crisis. This type of purely conceptual parallel, which a literary scholar recognizes instantly but that no word-matching system can detect, opens a window onto allusions that operate through shared situation rather than shared diction. It is also the type of detection that remains most experimental: the semantic channel's coverage is broad but its precision per pair is lower than lexical channels, and the interpretation of high cosine similarity scores in terms of literary significance remains an open question.

##### The Limits of Lexical Detection: Vergil's Plague and Lucretius

Thomas (1986, 179–180) demonstrates the range of non-lexical means by which Vergil's plague narrative in *Georgics* 3 recalls the Athenian plague in Lucretius *DRN* 6, including rare adverbial forms and syntactical imitation. Running a fusion search on Vergil's *Georgics* against Lucretius *DRN* 6 provides a diagnostic test of the system's strengths and blind spots.

The system finds extensive parallels between the two plague sections (*Georgics* 3.440–566 and *DRN* 6.1090–1286), with over 1,200 result pairs detected on two or more channels, confirming the dense lexical relationship. Shared vocabulary including *ulcus*, *sanguis*, *fauces*, *frigidus*, *pellis*, *genus*, *ferarum*, and *sacer ignis* produces strong matches, with the top plague result at rank 8. The system detects the relationship through multiple independent channels — lemma, exact, edit distance, dictionary, and semantic — providing the kind of multi-channel convergence that characterizes genuine intertextual engagement.

However, two of Thomas's specific examples initially exposed diagnostic problems that prompted targeted improvements. The rare adverb *minutatim*, shared between *Georgics* 3.485 and *DRN* 6.1191, appears in only 55 texts in the Latin corpus (IDF = 3.26) — a strong rarity signal. Yet the system initially ranked the pair at position 1080. The rare word channel, designed for exactly this scenario, failed to fire because its document-frequency threshold was set at 50, and *minutatim* falls just above at 55. The match was rescued by a two-line window that pairs *minutatim* with the common verb *traho* (IDF = 0.52), enabling the lemma channel, but the common word pulled down the geometric-mean IDF and suppressed the score. The rarer adverb *cateruatim* (doc_freq = 52, shared between *Georgics* 3.556 and *DRN* 6.1144) fared worse: rank ~14,282. It too missed the rare word threshold by 2, and the only other shared lemma, *do* (*dat*/*dabantur*), was filtered by a minimum-length check. As a single-word match, *cateruatim* received the single-word penalty (0.12, squared to 0.0144), reducing its score to near zero despite being one of the most distinctive lexical links between the two plague narratives. Raising the rare word threshold from 50 to 100 addressed both cases without regression: *minutatim* improved from rank 1080 to 54 (now found via a two-line window pairing it with *traho*), and *cateruatim* from ~14,282 to 655.

Thomas's most celebrated example, however, revealed a deeper challenge — and ultimately prompted a structural addition to the system. *Georgics* 3.481 (*corrupitque lacus, infecit pabula tabo*, "it tainted the pools, infected the pastures with corruption") and *DRN* 6.1140 (*vastavitque vias, exhausit civibus urbem*, "it devastated the streets, drained the city of citizens") share not a single word or lemma. Thomas observes that "the reminiscence is incontrovertible" — the tricolon structure (*Verb-que* + object, asyndetic verb + object + oblique) is identical, and the semantic parallelism (agents of pestilential destruction acting on spaces and their inhabitants) is unmistakable. Three channels came close to detecting it:

1. Semantic. The SPhilBERTa cosine similarity between the two lines is 0.579 — above the 0.5 detection threshold. But *DRN* 6.1140 ranks only 280th among all DRN 6 lines for similarity to *Georgics* 3.481. With 741 DRN 6 lines exceeding the threshold (plague and destruction vocabulary saturates the channel), the per-source-line cap of 100 targets filters it out.

2. Syntax. LatinPipe's dependency parses reveal that both lines have identical head structures: `[0, 1, 4, 1, 4, 4, 1]` — the tricolon pattern Thomas describes is captured perfectly in the parse data. But the syntax channel uses lemma-based pruning to avoid evaluating all O(*n* × *m*) line pairs: it only compares lines that share at least one lemma. Since these lines share zero lemmas, their structural similarity is never computed.

3. Dictionary. The V3 synonym database contains no entries linking *corrumpo*/*vasto* or *inficio*/*exhaurio*, though both pairs are semantically related (both describe acts of destruction/depletion). The curated resource, drawn from Lewis and Short, simply does not include these particular synonym relationships.

Each filtering decision — the rare word threshold, the semantic cap, the syntax pruning, the dictionary's coverage — was individually justifiable for performance or precision reasons. Together, they created a blind spot for exactly the kind of allusion Thomas describes: structural imitation with complete lexical substitution. The diagnosis drove three targeted fixes. First, a structural fingerprint path was added to the syntax channel: lines with identical dependency head patterns are now matched directly, bypassing the lemma requirement, with negligible performance cost (the fingerprint index is highly selective, adding fewer than 1,000 candidate pairs per search). Second, a semantic recovery step was added for structural matches: after all channels run, the system looks up the actual cosine similarity for any structural pair that was filtered by the semantic channel's per-source-line cap, allowing fusion to combine syntactic and semantic evidence. Third, the synonym *corrumpo*↔*vasto* was added to the V6 dictionary expansion, providing lexical confirmation for the pair.

However, structural pattern matching alone proved far too noisy: many unrelated Latin lines share common dependency patterns (e.g., subject-verb-object lists, participial phrases), and moderate SPhilBERTa cosine similarity (0.5–0.6) between any two Latin hexameter lines is typical rather than diagnostic. In initial testing, fewer than one in four structural matches represented meaningful parallels. To address this, a two-tier confirmation gate was added: structural fingerprint pairs are kept only if they have (a) at least one dictionary synonym pair between the two lines, confirming lexical as well as structural similarity, or (b) high cosine similarity (≥ 0.70), indicating strong semantic correspondence even without shared vocabulary. Pairs meeting neither criterion are discarded. This gate eliminated approximately 90% of false positives from the structural channel while preserving dictionary-confirmed pairs like *Georgics* 3.481 / *DRN* 6.1140, where the *corrumpo*↔*vasto* synonym provides the lexical confirmation that the structural and semantic channels alone cannot. In a book-specific search (*Georgics* 3 vs. *DRN* 6), the pair now appears at rank 35; against the full works (*Georgics* vs. *DRN*, approximately 16 million line pairs), it ranks 474th — detected through the convergence of structural, semantic, and dictionary evidence.

To verify these thresholds, we examined all 129 raw structural fingerprint candidates from the *Georgics* 3 / *DRN* 6 search. Of the 55 pairs falling in the 0.50–0.70 cosine range with no dictionary confirmation — the "just below threshold" zone — none represented a meaningful scholarly parallel. Typical examples paired lines sharing a common syntactic frame (e.g., participial + noun + prepositional phrase) but with entirely unrelated content: kingfisher shores with lightning storms (cosine 0.69), horse legs with bird wings (0.66), beards with damp clothes (0.63). The 61 pairs below cosine 0.50 were similarly noise. Among the 8 dictionary-confirmed pairs, 5 were confirmed only by function words (*non*∼*et*, *ne*∼*non*) and received appropriately low scores from the rarity system; only the Thomas tricolon had a content-word dictionary match.

Pairs like Georg. 3.276 / DRN 6.537 (cosine 0.65) illustrate why the gate is necessary. Vergil's *saxa per et scopulos et depressas convallis* ("over rocks and crags and sunken valleys") and Lucretius's *supter item ut supera ventosis undique plenam* ("below likewise as above, full of windy [caves] on all sides") share an identical dependency structure — coordinated prepositional and adjectival phrases enumerating terrain features — and moderate semantic similarity reflecting their common subject of physical landscape. But the parallel is thematic rather than allusive: neither poet is echoing the other's specific passage, and the coincidence of syntactic frame does not rise to the level of intertextual engagement. At cosine 0.65, the pair correctly falls below the 0.70 threshold and is filtered out. The Thomas tricolon, by contrast, is preserved because dictionary confirmation (*corrumpo*↔*vasto*) provides the additional lexical evidence that distinguishes deliberate allusion from coincidental structural similarity.
### Greek

The same fusion architecture runs on Greek with no modification. We evaluated it against a benchmark from Hunter's (1989) commentary on Apollonius of Rhodes, *Argonautica* Book 3: 448 annotated parallels between Apollonius and Homer (*Iliad* and *Odyssey*), compiled by Emily Baer under Katie Lamberto (UB Classics, Spring 2012) and archived by the Tesserae project since 2013.

Following the same type conventions used in the Latin benchmarks (Coffee et al. 2012), the 448 entries are classified by strength: type 5 (near-verbatim quotation, 14 entries), type 4 (clear allusion with identifiable lexical connection, 107), type 3 (thematic parallel or structural echo, 235), and type 2 (loose connection, 92). We use type 4+5 as the gold standard, yielding 121 strong parallels — 53 from the *Iliad* (across 20 books) and 68 from the *Odyssey* (across 20 books). An important limitation is that the collation covers only *Argonautica* 3 lines 1–533 of approximately 1,407 total lines — roughly the first third of the book.

The evaluation uses the same absolute recall methodology as the Latin benchmarks: a gold pair is counted as found if any result in the full ranked output matches the gold source and target references (within ±3 lines for the target). Because both texts are Greek, the full ten-channel fusion pipeline is available: edit distance, sound similarity, exact match, lemma overlap, dictionary synonyms, SPhilBERTa semantic similarity, rare words, syntax, and single-lemma matching, plus four window channels for enjambment detection. The complete search ran across 40 Homer books containing gold pairs, totaling approximately 12 minutes of processing.

| Benchmark | Gold | Found | Recall |
|-----------|------|-------|--------|
| Homer *Iliad* | 53 | 36 | 67.9% |
| Homer *Odyssey* | 68 | 48 | 70.6% |
| Total | 121 | 84 | 69.4% |

Absolute recall is 69.4%, compared with 91.9% for Latin. The difference reflects the current state of Greek NLP infrastructure rather than a limitation of the architecture. Greek lemmatization relies on CLTK's backoff lemmatizer rather than the large curated lookup table available for Latin (61,000+ entries from UD treebanks), and Greek morphology — augments, contract verbs, multiple accent patterns — makes accurate lemmatization harder. The SPhilBERTa embedding model was trained on parallel biblical and patristic texts; while the Greek training data is substantial (the Septuagint and Greek New Testament), the Latin corpus is larger and more diverse. The Greek syntax database, built from Stanza's `grc_proiel` parser, covers 655 texts (161,060 lines), including all Homer and Apollonius texts, though structural fingerprint matches are sparser than in Latin — typically 4–19 candidates per book pair, with 0–2 passing the confirmation gate.

The results are consistent between the two Homeric poems: *Iliad* recall is 67.9% (36/53 gold pairs) and *Odyssey* recall is 70.6% (48/68 gold pairs). Both poems have full syntax database coverage, confirming that the syntax channel's contribution to Greek recall is marginal — likely because Greek syntactic parsing at the accuracy needed for fingerprint matching remains challenging.

Two examples illustrate what the system captures and what it misses. *Iliad* 13.339 (ἔφριξεν δὲ μάχη φθισίμβροτος ἐγχείῃσιν, "the man-destroying battle bristled with spears") and *Argonautica* 3.1355 (φρίξεν δὲ περὶ στιβαροῖς σακέεσσι δούρασιν, "it bristled around the stout shields with spears") share the distinctive verb φρίσσω and the weaponry vocabulary that Hunter identifies as a deliberate reworking of the Iliadic battlefield description; the lemma and exact channels detect the shared forms directly. By contrast, *Iliad* 14.166 (ἐς θάλαμον, "to the bedchamber") and *Argonautica* 3.8 (θαλάμόνδε, "bedchamber-ward") share a thematic connection — Hera entering her chamber to plot — but the single shared root θάλαμ- in different morphological forms may not survive lemmatization, and the interpretive link depends on recognizing that both passages stage the same narrative scenario (a goddess retreating to scheme). Most of the 37 missed gold pairs follow this second pattern: Hunter identifies a shared narrative motif, scene type, or character dynamic without sufficient shared vocabulary for lexical channels to detect. They represent the natural ceiling for lexical-dominant detection systems.

Precision at practical ranks is substantially lower than for Latin. Because the evaluation searches each source poem in its entirety — the *Iliad* (15,683 lines) and *Odyssey* (12,107 lines) — against *Argonautica* 3, the total result space approaches one million pairs, compared to the 300,000–380,000 typical of the Latin benchmarks (which use individual books as source texts). With only 121 gold pairs spread across this larger space, P@K is expected to be low:

| Benchmark | P@10 | P@50 | P@100 | P@1000 | P@5000 |
|-----------|------|------|-------|--------|--------|
| Homer *Iliad* | 0% | 0% | 0% | 0.1% | 0.2% |
| Homer *Odyssey* | 10% | 4% | 3% | 0.5% | 0.4% |

The *Odyssey* shows some precision at top ranks (P@10 = 10%), while the *Iliad* has no gold pairs in the top 1,000 results. This discrepancy likely reflects the nature of Apollonius's engagement with the two poems: the *Argonautica* 3 is a love story that draws more directly on the *Odyssey*'s domestic and emotional vocabulary (Circe, Calypso, Penelope) than on the *Iliad*'s battlefield language, making the *Odyssey* parallels more lexically recoverable. The low precision figures reinforce the conclusion that Greek ranking quality depends critically on improved lemmatization and richer synonym resources — the same architecture that achieves P@50 of 22–42% on Latin is constrained by the quality of upstream Greek NLP.

The lower recall compared to Latin reflects the state of Greek NLP resources — lemmatization quality and dictionary coverage — rather than architectural limitations, and these are areas of active improvement.

### English

The corpus includes 13 English texts — Milton, Shakespeare, Wordsworth, Browning, Cowper, Carroll, Swift, and selections from the World English Bible — and the full ten-channel fusion pipeline runs on English with NLTK WordNet lemmatization replacing the Latin and Greek lemmatizers. No gold-standard benchmark of English intertextual parallels comparable to the Latin commentarial tradition or the Hunter collation was available to us, so we present illustrative results rather than recall figures; creating appropriate benchmarks — perhaps drawing on scholarship documenting Milton's classical borrowings or Shakespeare's biblical allusions — remains future work.

A search of Milton's *Paradise Lost* (10,565 lines) against Shakespeare's *Hamlet* (4,058 lines) produces results that are immediately recognizable to readers of both texts. The top-ranked pairs include shared rare vocabulary (*ambitious*, *contracted brow*, *roof fretted*, *impetuous haste*) and shared collocations that reflect the thematic overlap between two works concerned with ambition, damnation, and the nature of the soul. Three examples illustrate the range:

- *Paradise Lost* 9.503 (*Floted redundant: pleasing was his shape*) and *Hamlet* II.2.615 (*the devil hath power to assume a pleasing shape*): the shared phrase *pleasing shape* links Satan's serpentine disguise in Eden to Hamlet's fear that the Ghost may be a devil in attractive form. Detected by six channels including semantic similarity.

- *Paradise Lost* 1.717 (*The Roof was fretted Gold*) and *Hamlet* II.2.318 (*this majestical roof fretted with golden fire*): the rare collocation *roof fretted* (appearing in only 3 of the 13 English texts) connects Pandaemonium's architecture to Hamlet's famous meditation on the world as theater. Detected by four channels.

- *Paradise Lost* 5.105 (*She forms Imaginations, Aerie shapes*) and *Hamlet* III.1.138 (*imagination to give them shape*): the pairing of *imagination* with *shape* links Eve's dream-visions to Hamlet's catalog of human faculties, both passages exploring the mind's power to generate forms. Detected by three channels.

These results suggest that the fusion architecture transfers effectively to English, where the combination of exact matching, lemmatization, phonetic similarity, and semantic embeddings captures both direct verbal echoes and subtler thematic connections. The absence of an English synonym dictionary (the V3 Latin and Greek synonym resources have no English equivalent) limits the system to six of the ten channels; adding an English synonym resource would likely improve both detection and ranking. We give this area less space not because English results are less promising but because without established benchmarks, the results cannot be evaluated with the same rigor applied to the Latin and Greek cases. This is an area that could be tested and built out further.

### Greek-Latin

#### Epic Incipits

Hinds (2020) issued a direct challenge to computational intertext detection: the dialogue between Greek and Latin literature, foundational to the classical tradition, has "largely eluded computational formalization." He demonstrated the point through a close analysis of Greco-Roman epic openings, showing how the Roman poets transformed, compressed, and reframed the incipits of the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* through strategies ranging from near-literal rendering to genealogical substitution to phonetic punning. This section reports on our response to that challenge, extending the fusion methodology described above to the cross-lingual case. Just as the Latin fusion system combines independent channels — lexical, phonetic, semantic, syntactic — to detect parallels that no single channel captures alone, cross-lingual fusion combines semantic embeddings with dictionary matching to detect Greek-Latin intertexts that neither channel finds reliably on its own.

Five incipit pairs from Hinds's analysis provide a graduated test, ranging from near-translation to creative transformation:

1. Homer, *Iliad* 1.1 (*μῆνιν ἄειδε θεὰ Πηληϊάδεω Ἀχιλῆος*) → Ilias Latina 1 (*Iram pande mihi Pelidae, Diua, superbi*): a near-literal rendering where μῆνιν becomes *iram*, θεά becomes *diua*, and Πηληϊάδεω becomes *Pelidae*.
2. Homer, *Odyssey* 1.1 (*ἄνδρα μοι ἔννεπε, μοῦσα, πολύτροπον*) → Vergil, *Aeneid* 1.1 (*Arma virumque cano*): ἄνδρα becomes *virum* and ἔννεπε becomes *cano*, with *arma* added as Vergil's distinctive contribution.
3. Homer, *Iliad* 1.1 → Statius, *Achilleid* 1.1 (*magnanimum Aeaciden formidatamque Tonanti*): the patronymic shifts from Πηληϊάδης ("son of Peleus") to *Aeaciden* ("grandson of Aeacus"), a genealogical reframing that requires mythological knowledge to connect.
4. Homer, *Iliad* 1.1 → Vergil, *Aeneid* 1.37 (*Mene incepto desistere victam*): Hinds identifies a phonetic pun where *Mene* echoes μῆνιν, a purely aural allusion with no semantic or lexical connection.
5. Apollonius, *Argonautica* 1.1 (*Ἀρχόμενος σέο, Φοῖβε, παλαιγενέων κλέα φωτῶν*) → Valerius Flaccus, *Argonautica* 1.5 (*Phoebe, mone*): Φοῖβε becomes *Phoebe*, an invocation to Apollo that frames both Argonautic poems.

Using the SPhilBERTa semantic channel alone — which operates on cross-lingual embeddings trained on parallel Greek-Latin texts — the system detects semantic affinity for all five pairs. But ranks are measured per-source-line: given a single Greek opening line, how many of the hundreds of Latin lines in the target book outscore the correct match? Some pairs rank well on semantics alone, but others are lost in the noise:

| Pair | Semantic rank | Cosine |
|------|:---:|:---:|
| A.R. 1.1 / VF 1.5 | 1 | 0.754 |
| Il. 1.1 / IL 1 | 2 | 0.699 |
| Od. 1.1 / Aen. 1.8 (Musa) | 4 | 0.713 |
| Od. 1.1 / Aen. 1.1 | 429 | 0.481 |
| Il. 1.1 / Aen. 1.37 (Mene) | 451 | 0.500 |
| Il. 1.1 / Ach. 1.1 | 571 | 0.530 |

The top three pairs have strong cosine similarity that places them near the top. But the *Odyssey* 1.1 / *Aeneid* 1.1 connection — arguably the most famous of all — ranks 429th because its low cosine (0.481) is outscored by hundreds of other Latin lines with generic thematic similarity to the *Odyssey*'s opening. The Achilleid and phonetic-pun pairs fare even worse.

To improve cross-lingual detection, we developed a two-channel fusion that combines the semantic embeddings with a dictionary channel for Greek-Latin word matching. The dictionary draws on four layers: (1) over 900 curated entries for common cross-lingual pairs including divine epithets (φοῖβος → *phoebus*), Muse invocations (μοῦσα → *musa*), patronymics (Πηληϊάδης → *pelides*, *aeacides*), epic vocabulary (ἐνέπω → *cano*, *memoro*), and compound adjectives (μεγάθυμος → *magnanimus*); (2) the V3 Lewis and Short / LSJ bilingual dictionary (34,536 entries); (3) a proper name gazetteer of over 1,500 Greek-Latin pairs compiled from Wikidata mythological entities, the Pleiades ancient world gazetteer, and manual curation of major epic figures and Olympian deities with their Roman equivalents; and (4) cognate detection via Greek-to-Latin transliteration. The fusion formula combines semantic and dictionary scores with a convergence bonus when both channels fire:

> fused = 1.2 × cosine + 2.0 × dict_score × √(match_count) + 0.5 (if both channels fire)

The results are substantially better:

| Pair | Semantic rank | Fusion rank | Dict matches |
|------|:---:|:---:|---|
| Il. 1.1 / IL 1 | 2 | 1 | 4: μῆνις→*ira*, θεά→*diua*, Πηληϊάδης→*pelides*, Ἀχιλλεύς→*pelides* |
| Od. 1.1 / Aen. 1.8 | 4 | 1 | 2: ἐνέπω→*memoro*, μοῦσα→*musa* |
| Od. 1.1 / Aen. 1.1 | 429 | 2 | 2: ἀνήρ→*uir*, ἐνέπω→*cano* |
| A.R. 1.1 / VF 1.5 | 1 | 1 | 1: φοῖβος→*phoebus* + phonetic |
| Il. 1.1 / Ach. 1.1 | 571 | 22 | 2: Πηληϊάδης→*aeaciden*, Ἀχιλλεύς→*aeaciden* + phonetic |
| Il. 1.1 / Aen. 1.37 | 451 | — | 0 (phonetic only — detected but excluded from ranking) |

Five of the six pairs rank in the top 30 of their respective per-source-line searches (for a given Greek line, how many Latin lines in the target book outrank the correct match). The most dramatic improvement is *Odyssey* 1.1 / *Aeneid* 1.1: from semantic rank 429 to fusion rank 2, as the dictionary identifies exactly the two word-pairs (ἀνήρ→*uir*, ἐνέπω→*cano*) that every reader of Vergil recognizes. The Ilias Latina opening reaches rank 1 with four dictionary matches confirming the near-literal translation that Hinds describes.

The *Achilleid* 1.1 pair (rank 22) illustrates how the dictionary handles patronymic variation. Statius's *Aeaciden* ("grandson of Aeacus") does not share a lemma with Homer's Πηληϊάδης ("son of Peleus"), but both are patronymics for Achilles. The cross-lingual dictionary maps all three Greek names for Achilles (Ἀχιλλεύς, Πηληϊάδης, Αἰακίδης) to their Latin equivalents, enabling the system to recognize the genealogical substitution that Hinds identifies — though the match is to *aeaciden* rather than *achilles*, since that is the form Statius uses. The phonetic channel further reinforces this pair: transliterating Πηληϊάδεω to "peleiadeō" yields a partial edit-distance match against Latin patronymic forms, adding convergence credit.

The addition of a cross-lingual phonetic channel (see "Scaling Cross-Lingual Detection" below) also improved rankings for two pairs. The Apollonius / Valerius Flaccus pair rose from rank 7 to rank 1, as transliterated Greek forms reinforced the single dictionary match. The *Achilleid* pair improved from rank 26 to rank 22 for similar reasons. The phonetic channel also detects the *Mene*/μῆνιν echo: transliterating μῆνιν yields "menin," which has 67% edit similarity to *Mene*. However, because this is a phonetic-only match with no semantic or dictionary support, the pair is excluded from the ranked output entirely — phonetic-only pairs are too noisy to include. The system can now identify the phonetic relationship that Hinds describes, but it cannot yet rank a purely aural allusion without corroborating evidence from other channels.

A second example illustrates the two channels working together on a passage with deeper narrative content. The famous parallel between Vergil's *Aeneid* 6.701 and Homer's *Odyssey* 11.207, describing the futile attempt to embrace a shade, is one that Macrobius (*Saturnalia* 5.4) himself cites as an instance of Vergilian imitation:

> Vergil: *ter frustra comprensa manus effugit imago* — "Three times the image fled his grasping hands in vain"
>
> Homer: τρὶς δέ μοι ἐκ χειρῶν σκιῇ εἴκελον ἢ καὶ ὀνείρῳ — "And three times it slipped from my hands like a shadow or dream"

No vocabulary is shared between Greek and Latin, yet the semantic correspondence is rich: *ter* echoes τρίς ("three times"), *manus* echoes χειρῶν ("hands"), *imago* echoes σκιή ("shadow"). The SPhilBERTa embeddings capture this conceptual alignment, producing a cosine similarity of 0.687 — high enough to rank the pair 7th among all 901 lines of *Aeneid* 6 when scored on semantics alone. The dictionary identifies one direct lexical correspondence: χείρ → *manus* ("hand"). With both channels combined plus the convergence bonus, the pair ranks 22nd per source line — high given the density of underworld vocabulary shared between *Odyssey* 11 and *Aeneid* 6 (the dictionary match σκιή → *umbra* fires on dozens of Aeneid 6 lines describing shades). The single dictionary match on χείρ → *manus*, precisely targeting the one word-pair that has a direct translation equivalent beyond the pervasive shadow vocabulary, provides discriminating signal within this thematically saturated book pair.

The principle at work is the same one that drives the Latin fusion system: independent channels, each capturing a different dimension of the intertextual signal, produce stronger evidence in combination than either achieves alone. The semantic channel provides broad coverage across the language boundary; the dictionary channel provides lexical precision for specific word correspondences; and the convergence bonus rewards pairs where both signals agree. The cross-lingual fusion has since been extended to four channels — adding cross-lingual syntax and phonetic transliteration (described below) — though it still lacks the full ten-channel architecture and the benchmark-driven weight optimization of the Latin system. Nevertheless, it demonstrates a direct response to Hinds's challenge: the computational formalization of Greek-Latin intertextuality is no longer beyond reach. On the Tesserae website, all four channels run as a single unified search: users select a Greek source and Latin target, and the system automatically runs all channels and fuses the results, with a "Min Dictionary Matches" filter that allows narrowing to vocabulary-confirmed pairs.

#### Knauer's Iliad-Aeneid Parallels

The Hinds incipit test, while illuminating, involves only a handful of carefully selected passages. To assess cross-lingual detection at scale, we turned to the comprehensive catalog of parallels between Vergil's *Aeneid* Book 1 and Homer's *Iliad* compiled by Knauer (1964). This benchmark comprises 412 unique parallel pairs spanning all 24 books of the *Iliad*, covering the full range from near-literal translations to subtle thematic echoes.

The evaluation uses the same absolute recall methodology as the Latin benchmarks. With the full 4-channel pipeline (semantic embeddings, dictionary, cross-lingual syntax, and phonetic transliteration), the system detects 389 of 412 gold pairs (94.4%) at any rank — comparable to the Latin system's 91.9%. However, the challenge is ranking: most gold pairs are buried among hundreds of thousands of results with comparable scores. R@1,000 = 2.9%, R@5,000 = 10.0%. The system finds the right pairs but cannot reliably rank them above non-gold pairs.

The progression from semantic-only detection to multi-channel fusion illustrates the value of each signal. With SPhilBERTa semantic embeddings alone, the system shows the same ~20–35% recall plateau observed across embedding-based approaches (cf. Manjavacas et al. 2019, P@10 of 18–25% on Bernard-Vulgate). Adding the four-layer dictionary as a second channel substantially improved recall. The strongest matches were pairs with dictionary evidence: πατὴρ ἀνδρῶν τε θεῶν τε → *divom pater atque hominum rex* ranked first, driven by multiple dictionary hits.

Analysis of the 289 missed pairs revealed a clear pattern: the overwhelming majority had zero dictionary matches and cosine similarities of 0.5–0.7, outranked by other lines with similar semantic scores. The problem was not that the embedding model failed to detect similarity, but that it could not differentiate the target from the many thematically related but non-parallel lines in a given book. This diagnosis suggested targeted dictionary expansion as the most effective intervention.

Targeted dictionary expansion was the most effective intervention. Four rounds added over 900 new entries to the curated Greek-Latin dictionary, increasing it from 222 to 925 entries covering the core shared vocabulary of Homeric and Vergilian epic: gods and heroes, body and mind, warfare and weapons, nature and geography, ships and sailing, emotions and mental states, speech and thought, movement, and formulaic epithets. Round 1 (+57 entries) focused on categories revealed by miss analysis: body and person terms (δάκρυ→*lacrima*, γένος→*genus*, παρθένος→*virgo*), warfare (τεῖχος→*moenia*, χαλκός→*ferrum*), nature (ἄνεμος→*ventus*, λαῖλαψ→*procella*), and action verbs (βαίνω→*eo*, κλαίω→*fleo*), improving recall from 123 to 143 pairs (34.7%). Rounds 2–3 added family terms, emotion verbs, and a broad expansion across 23 semantic fields (+540 entries combined), reaching 151 pairs (36.7%). Round 4 (+297 entries) took the most systematic approach, extracting every Greek and Latin lemma from all 412 gold pairs and identifying genuine translation equivalences verified against standard lexica, reaching 164 pairs (39.8%). The diminishing returns across rounds — +20, +5, +3, +13 — confirmed that lexical coverage was approaching its practical ceiling: after four rounds of expansion, only 31.1% of Knauer's gold pairs have any dictionary match, up from approximately 12% initially. The remaining 69% are predominantly thematic and narrative parallels expressed through different vocabulary: conceptual echoes (Latin *dolens* for Greek χολωθείς, "angered"), structural borrowings with no shared words, and culturally specific vocabulary too divergent for any general-purpose bilingual dictionary.

Using absolute recall — the same methodology used for the Latin benchmarks, where all results from the full 4-channel pipeline (semantic, dictionary, syntax, phonetic) are collected and ranked globally — the system detects 389 of 412 gold pairs (94.4%) at any rank. This channel coverage is comparable to the Latin system's 91.9% (792/862), indicating that the cross-lingual pipeline successfully identifies nearly all of Knauer's parallels on at least one channel. However, precision at rank is low: R@1,000 = 2.9% (12/412), R@5,000 = 10.0% (41/412), R@10,000 = 15.3% (63/412). The gap between 94.4% detection and poor ranking reflects the fundamental difficulty of cross-lingual discrimination: the system finds the right pairs but cannot reliably rank them above the hundreds of thousands of non-gold pairs with comparable scores.

Why precision is so difficult becomes clear from inspection of Knauer's catalog, which is deliberately comprehensive: it records every connection he observed between the *Iliad* and *Aeneid* 1, from near-translations (πατὴρ ἀνδρῶν τε θεῶν τε → *divom pater atque hominum rex*) to single-word conceptual links (πόνος → *labores*, κεῖται → *iacet*) to proper name correspondences (Ἕκτωρ → *Hector*, Ἰλιονῆα → *Ilionei*) to formulaic paraphrases (φέρτατ᾽ Ἀχαιῶν → *Danaum fortissime*) to narrative-structural parallels expressed through entirely different vocabulary. Many pairs share only a single content word or a proper name, making them indistinguishable at the scoring level from the many coincidental co-occurrences of the same vocabulary across two long epic poems. The 23 pairs (5.6%) not detected by any channel are exclusively narrative and structural parallels with no recoverable lexical or semantic signal — the absolute floor for any word-level detection system.

Cross-lingual syntax matching using Universal Dependencies has been integrated as a third channel. Because UD dependency relation labels (nsubj, obj, obl, etc.) are language-independent by design, the structural fingerprint matching already used in the Latin system can operate directly across the language boundary: when a Greek line and a Latin line share the same pattern of dependency head relations (e.g., both have nsubj→root←obj with matching tree depth), they are identified as a structural parallel regardless of vocabulary. To build the Greek side of this capability, all 655 Greek texts in the corpus (161,060 lines) were parsed using the Stanza `grc_proiel` dependency parser, producing a Greek syntax database analogous to the Latin one built with LatinPipe. The build required approximately 10 hours of processing time with checkpoint-based resumption.

In practice, cross-lingual structural fingerprint matching produces a small number of high-precision candidates per book pair — typically 4–30 novel fingerprint matches between an Iliad book and Aeneid 1, compared to thousands of semantic and dictionary pairs. When evaluated on the Knauer benchmark, the addition of syntax as a third channel did not change the 2-channel recall at rank 50: the matches are too sparse to shift rankings at this scale. However, the channel provides a qualitatively different kind of evidence — syntactic parallelism independent of vocabulary — that may prove more valuable for targeted analysis of specific passages or for text pairs with denser structural imitation than Homer-Vergil.

A fourth cross-lingual channel exploits the phonetic relationship between Greek and Latin. Greek tokens are transliterated to Latin characters using a standard mapping (α→a, β→b, γ→g, δ→d, ε→e, ζ→z, η→e, θ→th, ι→i, κ→c, λ→l, μ→m, ν→n, ξ→x, ο→o, π→p, ρ→r, σ/ς→s, τ→t, υ→u, φ→ph, χ→ch, ψ→ps, ω→o) and compared against Latin tokens by edit distance. This enables the detection of phonetic echoes across the script boundary — cases where a Latin word sounds like a Greek word without sharing its meaning. Because the channel is noisy in isolation (approximately 10% of all line pairs exceed the 60% edit similarity threshold), it functions as a convergence booster: phonetic matches add to the score of pairs already found by semantic or dictionary channels, but pairs detected only by the phonetic channel are excluded. The channel improved several Hinds incipit rankings, most notably promoting Apollonius *Argonautica* 1.1 / Valerius Flaccus *Argonautica* 1.5 from per-source-line rank 7 to rank 1 and *Iliad* 1.1 / *Achilleid* 1.1 from rank 26 to rank 22, as transliterated Greek names and invocations reinforced the dictionary and semantic signals. Across the full Knauer benchmark, the complete 4-channel pipeline (semantic, dictionary, syntax, phonetic) achieves per-target-line recall of 171/412 (41.5%) at rank 50, a modest improvement over the 2-channel semantic-and-dictionary baseline, driven primarily by phonetic convergence boosting on pairs that already have dictionary or semantic support.

The 94.4% absolute recall suggests that the detection problem is largely solved — the system finds the pairs — but the ranking problem remains open. Several approaches could improve cross-lingual precision. First, improved multilingual embeddings remain the most promising path to breaking through the 20–35% ceiling that current embedding models consistently reach when used as the sole detection signal. Our SPhilBERTa model (Riemenschneider and Frank 2023), trained on parallel Latin-Greek-English biblical and patristic texts, achieves cosine similarities of 0.50–0.70 for most gold pairs but cannot discriminate them from thematically similar non-parallel lines. Manjavacas et al. (2019) used word-level embeddings (word2vec and fastText) rather than sentence-level transformers, and reported similarly modest results for their hybrid lexical-semantic approach on medieval Latin. Newer multilingual sentence transformers (e.g., multilingual-e5, LaBSE) offer broader language coverage but are not trained on classical texts and may lack the domain specificity needed for precise discrimination — though Schelb et al. (2026) found that the general-purpose E5-large outperformed SPhilBERTa on Latin retrieval, suggesting domain specificity may matter less than model scale for the retrieval stage. Second, contrastive fine-tuning on known parallel pairs — using Knauer's catalog and similar gold-standard datasets as training data, with non-parallel lines from the same texts as hard negatives — could teach an embedding model to discriminate true parallels from thematically similar but unrelated lines. Third, a cross-encoder re-ranking stage could take the top candidates from the current pipeline and re-score them using full passage context, exploiting the ability of transformer models to attend jointly to both passages — an approach that Schelb et al. (2026) demonstrated successfully for Latin intertextuality, achieving 79% Recall@100. Fourth, named entity linking across languages (Ἕκτωρ → Hector, Ἰλιονῆα → Ilionei) could serve as an additional high-precision channel, since proper name correspondences are strong indicators of intentional allusion but currently receive no special treatment.


### Latin-English

The same cross-lingual architecture that supports Greek-Latin detection has been extended to English. This extension exploits two properties of the system's infrastructure. First, the SPhilBERTa embedding model (Riemenschneider and Frank 2023), though trained primarily on parallel Latin-Greek-English biblical and patristic texts, is built on the XLM-RoBERTa multilingual base and handles English well: empirical testing shows cosine similarities of 0.70–0.88 for related cross-lingual pairs (e.g., "Of arms and the man I sing" vs. *arma virumque cano*: 0.75) and 0.40 for unrelated pairs, providing useful discrimination. Second, the Perseus Digital Library's Dynamic Lexicon project (Bamman and Crane 2008) provides word-level translation probabilities derived from GIZA++ alignment of parallel texts — 59 Latin-English source texts. From these alignments we extracted 4,552 Latin-English dictionary entries, filtered by log-likelihood and occurrence count to remove noise. This dictionary serves as the second fusion channel, paralleling the role of the Greek-Latin dictionary in the original cross-lingual pipeline.

The English dictionary channel uses the same inverted-index architecture as the Greek-Latin path, achieving sub-second matching even for large text pairs (e.g., 756 × 10,565 lines in 0.5 seconds). An English-specific stoplist of 139 words — including archaic forms common in the corpus (*thou*, *thee*, *thy*, *doth*, *hath*, *unto*) — suppresses function-word noise. All cross-lingual language pairs share a unified fusion pipeline with language-appropriate normalization: accent stripping for Greek, v→u and j→i normalization for Latin, and NLTK WordNet lemmatization for English.

Preliminary results are encouraging. A search of Vergil's *Aeneid* Book 1 against Milton's *Paradise Lost* identifies plausible parallels: the top-ranked Latin-English result pairs Aeneid 1.21 (*bellum ... superbos*, "war ... the proud") with Paradise Lost 1.43 (*War ... proud*) at 63% semantic similarity plus two dictionary matches, capturing a thematic connection between Juno's wrath and Satan's rebellion. However, no established benchmark of Latin-English intertext parallels comparable to Knauer's catalog or the Latin commentarial tradition was found for this study. Creating appropriate evaluation benchmarks — perhaps drawing on scholarship documenting Milton's Vergilian borrowings — remains future work. Until such benchmarks exist, the Latin-English mode should be considered experimental.

### Greek-English

The Greek-English cross-lingual pipeline uses the same two-channel architecture (SPhilBERTa semantic embeddings plus dictionary matching) as the Greek-Latin and Latin-English paths. The dictionary draws on 118 Greek-English source texts from the Perseus Dynamic Lexicon, yielding 4,972 Greek-English entries after filtering. The 13 English texts currently in the corpus — Milton, Shakespeare, Browning, Cowper, and selections from the World English Bible — represent a modest but heterogeneous sample of English literature with varying degrees of classical engagement.

A search of Homer's *Odyssey* 11 (the Nekyia, Odysseus's descent to the underworld) against Milton's *Paradise Lost* illustrates the system's ability to detect thematic connections across the Greek-English boundary. Three examples from the top results:

- *Odyssey* 11.292 (χαλεπὴ δὲ θεοῦ κατὰ μοῖρα πέδησε, "a harsh doom of the god bound him") and *Paradise Lost* 3.224 (*By doom severe, had not the Son of God*): three dictionary matches — μοῖρα→*doom*, θεός→*god*, χαλεπός→*severe* — plus cosine similarity of 0.720. Both passages describe divine judgment constraining mortals: in Homer, the fate imposed on the sons of Aloeus; in Milton, the severe sentence on humanity averted only by the Son's intercession.

- *Odyssey* 11.307 (καί ῥ' ἔτεκεν δύο παῖδε, "and she bore two children") and *Paradise Lost* 3.463 (*Hither of ill-joynd Sons and Daughters born*): three dictionary matches — παῖς→*son*, παῖς→*daughter*, τίκτω→*bear* — plus cosine similarity of 0.703. The semantic channel detects the shared subject of divine or semi-divine offspring, while the dictionary identifies the specific vocabulary of parentage.

- *Iliad* 1.112 (οὐκ ἔθελον δέξασθαι, ἐπεὶ πολὺ βούλομαι αὐτήν, "they were unwilling to accept [the ransom], since I much desire her") and *Paradise Lost* 8.451 (*Thy wish, exactly to thy hearts desire*): four dictionary matches, as both Greek verbs for wanting (βούλομαι and ἐθέλω) map to both English words (*wish* and *desire*), and Milton's line uses both. Cosine similarity of 0.708 reflects the shared theme of desire and will.

As with Latin-English, no established benchmark of Greek-English intertext parallels comparable to Knauer's catalog or the Latin commentarial tradition was available for this study. Creating appropriate benchmarks — perhaps drawing on the rich tradition of biblical translation parallels or scholarship on Greek influence in English poetry — remains future work. Until such benchmarks exist, the Greek-English mode should be considered experimental.

## Discussion and Conclusion

The channel contribution analysis (see "Channel Contribution Analysis") suggests that Latin allusive practice is more multi-dimensional than lexical matching alone can reveal. Each non-lexical channel — phonetic, morphological, semantic, syntactic, dictionary — contributes unique gold-standard parallels that no other channel detects, confirming that these are not redundant but complementary dimensions of allusive technique.

These findings are consistent with the theoretical accounts of allusion offered by Conte (1986), Hinds (1998), and Machacek (2007), which emphasize the variety of strategies available to ancient poets. What the computational evidence adds is a sense of proportion: how often each strategy is used, across how many text pairs, and with what degree of consistency. The data suggest that no single strategy dominates, and that the "best" detection method depends on the allusive practice of the specific author under study. These patterns hold across the three languages tested, though with important differences in coverage and resource maturity.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The full ten-channel fusion has been developed and optimized primarily for Latin, though the Apollonius-Homer evaluation demonstrates that the architecture generalizes to Greek without modification. The lower Greek recall (69.4% vs. 91.9% for Latin) reflects the state of Greek NLP resources — Greek lemmatization relies on CLTK's backoff lemmatizer without the 62,000-entry curated lookup table available for Latin, and the SPhilBERTa embedding model has less diverse Greek training data — rather than architectural limitations. The Greek syntax database is now complete (655 texts, 161,060 lines), but the syntax channel contributes minimally to Greek recall. The cross-lingual system currently operates with four channels (semantic, dictionary, cross-lingual syntax, and phonetic transliteration), fewer than the ten available for monolingual search. The phonetic channel can detect sound correspondences across the Greek-Latin script boundary, but standalone phonetic matches cannot yet be ranked reliably: the channel is too noisy in isolation (approximately 10% of all line pairs exceed the similarity threshold) and functions only as a convergence booster for pairs already found by other channels. Even after four rounds of dictionary expansion to 925 entries, only 31.1% of Knauer's gold pairs have any dictionary match. The 4-channel pipeline achieves 94.4% absolute recall (389/412 pairs detected on at least one channel), comparable to the Latin system's 91.9%, but ranking precision remains low (R@1,000 = 2.9%) because the system cannot reliably distinguish true parallels from the hundreds of thousands of pairs with comparable scores. Approximately 69% of Knauer's gold pairs are thematic or narrative parallels expressed through different vocabulary — beyond the reach of lexical approaches, and difficult even for current embedding models to discriminate from non-parallel lines. Improving cross-lingual ranking — through contrastive fine-tuning, cross-encoder re-ranking, or named entity linking — is the key open challenge. The Knauer (1964) benchmark provides 412 gold-standard pairs for Aeneid 1 vs. the Iliad, and the Hunter (1989) Apollonius-Homer benchmark provides 121 strong parallels (type 4+5) for Greek-Greek evaluation, but comparable benchmarks for other text pairs in both languages are still needed. The Hunter benchmark covers only the first third of *Argonautica* 3 (lines 1–533 of ~1,407), so its results represent a partial view. Parallels that no commentator happened to note are invisible to the evaluation. The benchmark P@K figures are therefore lower bounds on actual precision.

The three-layer rarity scoring system, with its curated stoplist, convergence modulation, and piecewise multiplier curve, is more complex than the clean formulas of the original V3 scorer. This complexity was not designed but emerged through iterative response to empirical problems: asymmetric pairs, lemmatization gaps, cross-text-pair instability. Simpler formulations may exist. The system deliberately maximizes recall, accepting lower precision in the long tail of results. Precision drops rapidly beyond rank 50: at P@500, even the best-performing benchmark (VF-Vergil) shows only 15%. As discussed in "Precision and Discovery" above, these benchmark figures are lower bounds, since many non-gold results are defensible parallels not catalogued by commentators. The tool is intended to cast a wide net for scholarly browsing, not to produce a clean list of definitive allusions, and scholars must exercise judgment in evaluating lower-ranked results.

Related to this tradeoff, the stoplist-based approach to function-word detection is precise for the 90 Latin words on the list, but the boundary between function words and common content words remains debatable. Words like *ire* ("to go") and *dare* ("to give") are common verbs that could reasonably be classified either way. The current system errs on the side of inclusion: it is better to rank a marginal match slightly too high than to suppress a genuine allusion. The approximately 8% of Latin parallels that escape all ten channels are predominantly thematic or conceptual, connected by shared ideas rather than any recoverable linguistic signal. Approaches based on topic modeling, such as the Latent Semantic Indexing method explored in Scheirer, Forstall, and Coffee (2016), which recovered a significant fraction of commentator-attested parallels through distributional semantics at the document level, represent a complementary path for capturing these thematic connections. Integrating such methods as an additional channel within the fusion framework remains a promising direction.

Beyond these technical directions, the project raises broader questions about how AI tools are changing research practice. This article has traced two roles for AI in intertext study: as a detection feature (the SPhilBERTa semantic channel) and as a development tool (the AI coding assistants that built the platform). That the same underlying technology powers both reflects its generality, with significant implications for digital humanities: scholars with deep domain knowledge but limited programming experience can direct AI tools to build computational instruments tailored to their research questions, provided they invest in validation practices.

The trajectory from the original Tesserae system's approximately 27% recall (Coffee et al. 2012) to Version 6's 91.9% for Latin and 69.4% for Greek represents substantial progress in computational intertext detection for classical literature. The modular, multi-channel architecture means that the primary constraint on further improvement is not the scoring framework but the quality of upstream resources: more capable multilingual embedding models and expanded syntactic parsing for Greek would yield immediate gains within the existing architecture. The complete source code, evaluation infrastructure, and benchmark datasets are openly available, providing a foundation for researchers who wish to develop their own detection strategies or adapt the system to other literary traditions.

Whether computational tools fundamentally change how we study intertextuality or merely accelerate existing practices remains an open question. What the fusion system makes possible is exhaustive search at a scale that manual reading cannot achieve: every line of Lucan compared against every line of Vergil, across ten dimensions of similarity, in under two minutes. The resulting ranked list presents both valuable allusions as well as raw material for scholarly interpretation, material that includes discoveries that might otherwise require serendipitous close reading to identify.

The experience of building this system suggests broader lessons about AI-assisted research in the humanities. AI coding agents are powerful tools, but they are tools — highly useful when used responsibly, requiring the same care and critical judgment that any powerful instrument demands. At a time when research funding has contracted, individual humanities scholars can now undertake projects that would previously have required multiple grant cycles over many years. In this case, the PI needed essentially a university server and a $100-per-month AI coding subscription. But this was not a matter of simply asking questions and receiving complete, reliable answers. It required extensive testing, checking, and engagement — the iterative cycle of implementing, measuring, diagnosing, and revising. Through that process, a humanities researcher with deep domain knowledge but no programming experience produced a platform that quadruples the recall of the previous system across five benchmarks. This project had the benefit of many separate resources developed over years, including benchmark datasets, curated dictionaries, NLP models, a large digitized corpus, along with expertise developed over that time, all brought together synergistically. It seems likely however that even projects with less existing infrastructure can benefit from responsible agentic AI development, provided they invest in the requisite validation practices.

The platform, source code, and evaluation materials are freely available at the URLs given in the abstract.
